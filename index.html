The Macro Shift

$84–128T is moving
to a different
kind of investor.

Over the next two decades, an unprecedented generational wealth transfer is underway in the United States alone. Millennials and Gen Z are inheriting not just capital — but a fundamentally different relationship with it.

These investors consistently report wanting their capital to reflect personal values, support themes or missions, create community, and feel connected to real outcomes.

But traditional investing infrastructure remains designed for a different archetype: individual, financially-maximizing, and institutionally intermediated.

The result is a structural mismatch between how people want to invest and how investing currently works. This gap is widening every year.

The Structural Gap

No common
infrastructure
exists.

Despite the growth of collaborative investing, there is no shared infrastructure layer for discovering collaborative funds, forming new investment collectives, managing governance, or enabling pooled capital deployment.

Today, most collaborative vehicles are created ad hoc — informal angel groups, spreadsheet-managed collectives, private WhatsApp communities, manually structured SPVs.

🔍
Difficult to Discover
No marketplace or directory for existing collaborative vehicles.
⚙
Difficult to Start
Forming a new collective requires legal, operational, and governance setup from scratch.
⚠
Operationally Burdensome
Email threads, spreadsheets, and manual processes for coordination.
🚫
Inaccessible
New participants can't find or join existing groups. Most collaborators never find each other.

A different
economy needs
different
investors.

If we want a different economy tomorrow, we need different investors today.

The companies that shape the future are built by the capital that backs them early. Yet most people have historically had little influence over what that capital supports.

When we create infrastructure that allows the next generation to invest collaboratively — around shared values, missions, and communities — we begin to change the direction of capital itself. And when the direction of capital changes, so does the economy.

We've already seen how systems that amplify participation can transform outcomes. In New York, public matching funds dramatically increased the influence of small donors, reshaping who could compete in elections.

Finance works the same way. When more people have the ability to direct capital according to their beliefs, different companies are funded — and a different future gets built.

The operating system for collaborative investing. A marketplace where people don't invest alone — they invest with communities around shared values.
